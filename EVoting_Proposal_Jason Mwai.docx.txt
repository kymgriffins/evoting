Abstract
This project proposes the development of an electronic voting system integrated with manifesto tracking capabilities to enhance candidate accountability in electoral processes. Traditional voting systems, both manual and electronic, often lack mechanisms for voters to verify whether elected officials fulfill their campaign promises after assuming office. This gap contributes to voter apathy, reduced trust in democratic institutions, and poor governance outcomes.
The proposed system addresses these challenges by providing a secure, web-based platform where candidates can upload detailed manifestos, voters can cast their votes electronically, and all stakeholders can monitor the implementation status of campaign promises post-election. The system incorporates biometric authentication, blockchain-inspired audit trails, and real-time manifesto progress tracking to promote transparency and accountability throughout the electoral cycle.
The project aims to increase voter participation, promote transparency, and establish a culture of accountability among elected leaders, with an initial pilot at the Catholic University of Eastern Africa. This proposal outlines the research background, problem statement, objectives, comprehensive literature review, development methodology, and project schedule, resources, and budget for the proposed system.


Keywords: Electronic Voting, Manifesto Tracking, Candidate Accountability, SSADM, Biometric Authentication, Democratic Transparency.

CHAPTER ONE: INTRODUCTION
1.1  Background of the Study
Democratic elections form the cornerstone of governance systems worldwide, providing citizens with the opportunity to choose their representatives and to hold them accountable for the exercise of public authority. The integrity of electoral processes is fundamental to the legitimacy of government, and any shortcomings in these processes tend to erode public trust and civic participation. Traditional voting methods, both paper-based and electronic, have faced persistent challenges, including voter fraud, logistical inefficiencies, lack of transparency, and, critically, a growing disconnect between campaign promises and post-election performance of elected officials.
Electronic voting systems have emerged as a technological response to some of these challenges, offering improvements in efficiency, accuracy, accessibility, and verifiability. Countries such as Estonia, Brazil, and India have successfully implemented e-voting systems over several decades, each demonstrating that technology can enhance different dimensions of the electoral process. In Kenya, the Independent Electoral and Boundaries Commission (IEBC) adopted the Kenya Integrated Election Management System (KIEMS), which employs biometric voter identification and electronic result transmission. While KIEMS represents a significant step forward, it primarily addresses logistical aspects of voting rather than the broader accountability framework needed to sustain democratic governance.
The concept of manifesto tracking as a systematic accountability mechanism has gained increasing attention. A candidate's manifesto serves as a formal public commitment to voters, articulating policy intentions and developmental priorities. Once a candidate is elected, there is barely any structured, technology-enabled mechanism through which voters can monitor whether those commitments are being honoured. This accountability gap contributes significantly to voter disillusionment, declining civic engagement, and weakened democratic culture, particularly among younger, digitally literate populations.
The proposed system addresses this gap by integrating secure electronic voting with a manifesto presentation and post-election tracking module. By combining these two functions in a single platform, the system aims to transform the electoral process from a single-day event into an ongoing democratic engagement tool that strengthens the relationship between elected officials and their constituents. The Catholic University of Eastern Africa (CUEA) student government elections provide an appropriate and manageable pilot context for this innovation.
1.2  Problem Statement
Despite the adoption of electronic voting systems in various jurisdictions, a significant disconnect persists between the voting process and the subsequent accountability of elected officials. Existing e-voting systems primarily focus on secure vote capture and transmission, overlooking the critical need for mechanisms that enable voters to verify whether candidates fulfill their campaign promises after assuming office.
This gap manifests in several concrete ways:
* Voters often lack access to comprehensive, standardised, and comparable candidate manifesto information at the time of voting.
* After elections, there is no centralised, publicly accessible platform through which citizens can track the implementation of campaign promises.
* Elected officials have no structured institutional mechanism to report on manifesto progress, making proactive accountability difficult to demonstrate.
* The absence of structured accountability mechanisms contributes to voter apathy, declining trust in democratic institutions, and a culture of impunity among elected officials.
* Manual or informal tracking of manifesto promises is inefficient, prone to bias and selective interpretation, and fails to provide real-time, auditable updates.
Consequently, there is a compelling need for an integrated system that combines secure electronic voting with robust manifesto presentation and post-election tracking capabilities to enhance candidate accountability and promote informed citizen participation.
1.3  Main Objective
To develop an electronic voting system with integrated manifesto tracking capabilities that enhances candidate accountability and promotes transparency in electoral processes.
1.4  Specific Objectives
* To analyse the requirements for an integrated e-voting and manifesto tracking system through stakeholder consultations and review of existing systems.
* To design a secure electronic voting system that incorporates candidate manifesto presentation, voter authentication, and post-election accountability features.
* To implement a web-based system that enables secure voting, manifesto upload by candidates, and real-time tracking of manifesto implementation progress.
* To test the functionality, security, and usability of the developed system using appropriate testing methodologies including unit, integration, system, and user acceptance testing.
* To deploy and evaluate the system in a controlled pilot environment to assess its effectiveness in promoting candidate accountability.
1.5  Justification
This project addresses a critical gap in electoral systems by integrating accountability mechanisms directly into the voting platform. The proposed system offers significant benefits to multiple stakeholder groups:
For Voters: The system provides access to comprehensive, comparable candidate manifestos before voting, enabling fully informed electoral choices. Post-election, voters can monitor whether their elected representatives are fulfilling campaign promises, increasing civic engagement and institutional trust.
For Candidates: The platform offers a structured, standardised channel through which candidates can present their vision and commitments, and subsequently demonstrate accountability to their constituents by reporting on progress against manifesto items.
For Democratic Governance: By strengthening accountability mechanisms and reducing the disconnect between campaign promises and governance outcomes, the system contributes to the broader goal of deepening democratic culture and good governance through technology.
The system's novelty lies in the integration of voting and accountability functions within a single platform—an integration that existing e-voting systems have largely neglected.
1.6  Scope
The proposed system will encompass the following functional modules:
* User Management: Registration and authentication for voters, candidates, and system administrators.
* Voter Authentication: Multi-factor authentication incorporating biometric (fingerprint) verification and institutional ID confirmation to prevent duplicate voting and ensure voter eligibility.
* Candidate and Manifesto Management: Candidate profiles with categorised, detailed manifestos covering thematic areas such as education, healthcare, infrastructure, and student welfare.
* Voting Module: Secure electronic ballot casting with end-to-end encryption, blockchain-inspired audit trails, and vote verification mechanisms.
* Manifesto Tracking Dashboard: Real-time updates on post-election manifesto implementation, using status indicators (Not Started, In Progress, Completed, Delayed) with supporting evidence attachments.
* Reporting and Analytics: Generation of electoral statistics reports and manifesto tracking metrics accessible to all stakeholders.
The system will be developed as a web-based application accessible via desktop and mobile browsers. The initial pilot implementation will focus on student government elections at the Catholic University of Eastern Africa. The system is designed with scalability considerations for broader electoral applications but will not replace constitutional electoral processes—it is intended as a supplementary accountability and transparency tool.

2.2  Methodology of Literature Review
The literature review was conducted using a systematic and structured approach to ensure comprehensiveness, relevance, and academic rigor. The following methodology was applied:

Electronic databases searched include Google Scholar, IEEE Xplore, ACM Digital Library, ScienceDirect, and the African Journals Online (AJOL). 2.2.2  Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria
Literature was included if it:

Grey literature from electoral commissions and international development organisations was included due to its operational relevance. Studies primarily focused on offline paper-based systems without electronic components were excluded.


2.2.3  Analysis and Synthesis
Retrieved materials were subjected to thematic analysis, grouping findings into categories: historical development, system architecture and security, voter experience and participation, accountability mechanisms, and emerging technology trends. 2.3  History of the Research Topic
The evolution of voting technology spans several decades and reflects the broader trajectory of digital innovation in public sector services. Early automated voting employed punch-card ballots in the United States from the 1960s, introducing the concept of machine-assisted counting but retaining paper as the primary voting medium. The subsequent development of Direct Recording Electronic (DRE) machines in the 1970s and 1980s represented the first fully electronic vote recording systems, eliminating paper ballots altogether (Alvarez & Hall, 2015).
The most radical innovation came with Estonia's introduction of Internet voting (i-Voting) in 2005, leveraging a national digital identity infrastructure to allow citizens to vote from any internet-connected device. Estonia's system has been replicated or studied as a model by dozens of countries, establishing internet voting as a technically and institutionally feasible option (Gibson, 2021).
The integration of accountability mechanisms with voting systems is a more recent and less developed frontier. While campaign manifestos have long been part of electoral culture, systematic digital tracking of manifesto implementation gained academic and civic attention in the 2010s with the rise of open government initiatives and civic technology movements. Platforms such as Promise Tracker (Brazil), Manifesto Monitor (UK), and various national open government portals demonstrated the citizen demand for post-election accountability tools. However, these platforms were consistently developed independently of voting infrastructure, creating siloed systems that failed to leverage the trust and engagement built during the electoral cycle (Barrat, 2020).
The confluence of mature e-voting technology with the growing civic technology and open government movements now presents an opportunity to develop integrated systems that address the full democratic cycle—from candidate manifesto presentation through secure voting to post-election accountability tracking. This integration represents the central innovation of the proposed system.
2.4  Review of Related Prototypes and Systems
2.4.1  Case 1: Kenya Integrated Election Management System (KIEMS) – IEBC
The Kenya Integrated Election Management System (KIEMS) was deployed by Kenya's Independent Electoral and Boundaries Commission(IEBC) to modernise the country's electoral administration. The system integrates biometric voter identification—using fingerprint verification—with electronic transmission of polling station results to a central tallying centre. KIEMS was significantly expanded for the 2013, 2017, and 2022 general elections, each cycle refining the system based on operational lessons (IEBC, 2024).
Strengths: KIEMS reduces voter impersonation through biometric verification; accelerates result transmission compared to manual processes; maintains a centralised, deduplicated voter registration database; and enhances transparency in the national tallying process.
Weaknesses and Gaps: The system has experienced reliability challenges during peak election-day usage and has faced public trust concerns. Critically, it provides no manifesto presentation, no post-election tracking, no candidate performance monitoring, and no mechanisms for ongoing voter-candidate engagement beyond the voting event.

2.4.2  Case 2: Estonia's National i-Voting System
Estonia's internet voting system, operational since 2005, is widely regarded as the most mature and successful national internet voting implementation globally. Voters authenticate using their national digital identity cards or mobile ID credentials, cast encrypted votes through a secure online portal, and may revise their vote during the advance voting period. The system has been used in every national and local election since its introduction, with internet voting accounting for over 50% of votes cast in recent elections (Estonia National Electoral Committee, 2024).
Strengths: The system delivers exceptional voter convenience and accessibility; employs strong cryptographic security through digital ID authentication; has an extensive, transparent track record spanning two decades; and supports comprehensive public auditing of the voting process.
Weaknesses and Gaps: Candidate manifestos are hosted on entirely separate platforms, requiring voters to consult multiple sources. No post-election tracking functionality exists.

2.4.5  Case 5: Brazil's e-Voting System
Brazil pioneered national e-voting with the introduction of DRE machines in 1996, a programme that has grown and matured over nearly three decades. The system now combines DRE machines with biometric voter authentication and is noted for its rapid result delivery, with national tallies typically completed within hours of polls closing. Brazil's system serves a large and geographically diverse population, demonstrating the operational resilience of well-institutionalised electronic voting (Nunes & Silva, 2024).
Strengths: The system delivers very rapid results, enjoys high levels of voter confidence built over decades, maintains a comprehensive biometric voter registration database, and includes accessibility features for voters with disabilities.
Weaknesses and Gaps: Brazil's system does not offer internet-based or remote voting. Candidate manifestos are not integrated into the voting platform, limiting informed voting. There are no citizen engagement mechanisms beyond ballot casting, and no systematic tracking of campaign promise fulfilment exists within the electoral infrastructure.

2.5  Emerging Trends and Patterns
Analysis of the five reviewed systems, combined with wider literature, reveals several important trends shaping the future of electronic voting:
* Biometric Authentication: There is a clear and accelerating trend towards biometric voter identification—fingerprint, iris, and facial recognition—to enhance identity verification, reduce fraud, and improve voter confidence in the integrity of the electoral roll.
* Blockchain and Distributed Ledger Technology: Growing interest in blockchain for immutable, tamper-evident vote records is evident in academic literature and pilot projects, though large-scale production deployments remain limited (Ritter & Schmitz, 2021).
* Mobile and Internet Accessibility: Jurisdictions are increasingly exploring mobile-compatible and internet-based voting to expand accessibility and voter participation, particularly among younger and geographically dispersed populations.
* Transparency Mechanisms: The VVPAT in India exemplifies a broader demand for voter-verifiable audit trails that allow individuals to confirm their vote was recorded correctly, addressing concerns about electronic system trustworthiness.
* Cloud-Based Infrastructure: Cloud technologies are being adopted to improve the scalability, redundancy, and reliability of electoral systems, reducing dependence on fragile local hardware infrastructure.
Despite these advances, a consistent and striking pattern across all reviewed systems is the near-total absence of integrated accountability mechanisms that bridge the voting event with post-election governance. No reviewed system provides manifesto presentation, promise tracking, or candidate performance monitoring within the electoral platform. This represents a significant gap between the technology's current capabilities and the full potential of digital democracy.
2.6  Research Gap
The foregoing literature review clearly establishes a gap in existing e-voting systems: the absence of integrated accountability mechanisms that connect the voting process with post-election candidate performance tracking. Specifically, the gap encompasses four dimensions:
* Informed Voting Deficit: No current production e-voting system provides voters with standardised, comparable manifesto information within the voting interface, leaving voters to research candidates independently and inconsistently.
* Post-Election Accountability Void: Existing systems end their functionality at ballot counting, with no mechanism for systematic tracking of manifesto implementation or candidate performance.
* Candidate-Voter Communication Gap: There are no platforms within existing electoral systems that enable ongoing, structured engagement between elected officials and their constituents around the fulfilment of campaign commitments.
* Performance Monitoring Absence: No measurable, auditable metrics for evaluating candidate performance against campaign promises are embedded within electoral system architecture.
This gap presents a clear opportunity for innovation. The proposed integrated system addresses all four dimensions by combining secure electronic voting with a comprehensive manifesto presentation and post-election tracking module, designed within a single, coherent platform architecture.
2.7  Chapter Summary
This chapter has examined the historical evolution of electronic voting from punch-card systems through DRE machines to internet voting, and has analysed five operational systems: Kenya's KIEMS, Estonia's i-Voting, Sierra Leone's pilot, India's EVM, and Brazil's e-voting system. The analysis reveals that while these systems have made substantial contributions to electoral efficiency, security, and accessibility, they consistently and uniformly lack integrated accountability mechanisms connecting the voting event to post-election governance. The identified research gap—centred on the absence of manifesto presentation, tracking, and accountability features—provides the foundational justification for the proposed integrated system. Chapter Three will now outline the methodological approach to be employed in its development.



CHAPTER THREE: METHODOLOGY
3.1  Introduction
This chapter describes the methodology to be employed throughout the development lifecycle of the e-voting system with integrated manifesto tracking. The development process is structured around the Structured Systems Analysis and Design Methodology (SSADM), selected for its rigorous, phase-by-phase approach that is well-suited to systems with complex security, data integrity, and multi-stakeholder requirements. SSADM's emphasis on thorough requirements analysis, formal modelling of data and process flows, and documented deliverables at each phase provides a strong quality assurance framework appropriate for an electoral system where accuracy and trustworthiness are paramount.
The chapter is organised to address each key phase of the development lifecycle: requirement elicitation, system analysis, system design, implementation, testing, and deployment. Each section identifies the tools, techniques, and rationale for the methodological choices made.
3.2  Methodology of Requirement Elicitation
Requirement elicitation will be conducted using a mixed-methods approach combining qualitative and quantitative data collection to ensure a comprehensive and representative understanding of stakeholder needs, expectations, and constraints.
3.2.1  Interviews
Semi-structured interviews will be conducted with key stakeholders including student leaders and candidates, university electoral commission officials, IT administrators responsible for university systems, and a purposive sample of 30 student voters. Semi-structured interviews are chosen for their flexibility in allowing follow-up probing on unexpected issues while maintaining thematic consistency across participants (Creswell & Creswell, 2018). Interviews will be audio-recorded with participants' consent and transcribed for thematic analysis.
3.2.2  Questionnaires
Structured questionnaires will be distributed to a sample of 200 students selected using stratified random sampling across faculties and year groups to ensure representativeness. The questionnaire will employ Likert scales, multiple-choice questions, and targeted open-ended items to gather both quantitative preference data and qualitative insights. The instrument is provided in Appendix A. Quantitative data will be analysed using descriptive statistics (frequencies, means, percentages), while open-ended responses will be subjected to thematic analysis.
3.2.3  Document Analysis
Existing CUEA electoral regulations, student government guidelines, past election reports, and relevant national electoral legislation will be reviewed to identify procedural requirements, compliance constraints, and systemic boundaries that the new system must respect.
3.3  Methodology for System Analysis
System analysis will be conducted using SSADM tools and techniques, producing formal models that serve as the basis for system design. The following analytical artefacts will be developed:
System Workflow Diagram: A high-level process flow illustrating the overall sequence of system operations from user registration through voting to post-election tracking.

3.4.3  System Architecture
The system will employ a three-tier architecture: 
3.4.4  Security Design
Security architecture will include: role-based access control (RBAC) with three roles (Voter, Candidate, Administrator); AES-256 encryption for sensitive data at rest and in transit; JSON Web Token (JWT) stateless authentication; bcrypt password hashing; parameterised database queries to prevent SQL injection; Content Security Policy headers to prevent cross-site scripting; rate limiting to mitigate denial-of-service attacks; and comprehensive audit logging for all critical system operations.
3.5  Methodology for System Implementation
Implementation will be conducted following the structured, sequential plan established during the System Design phase of SSADM. The process will be executed in a methodical, controlled manner to ensure that all documented functional and non-functional requirements are met before proceeding to the testing phase.


Technology Stack:
- Frontend: React Js and CSS for structuring and styling the user interface, with JavaScript for client-side interactivity
- Backend: Python using the Django framework for robust application development, security, and Object-Relational Mapping (ORM) integration
- Database: PostgreSQL RDBMS for reliable data storage and management
- Version Control: Git with GitHub for collaborative version management, code review, and change tracking
- Development Tools: Visual Studio Code IDE, Postman for API testing, and browser developer tools for debugging


- Django best practices will be followed for project structure, security configurations, and ORM usage
- Code documentation will follow established conventions for maintainability
- Regular code reviews will ensure quality and consistency across the development team
3.6  Methodology for System Testing
A multi-level testing strategy will be employed to validate both functional and non-functional requirements:
* Unit Testing: Jest will be used to test individual components and functions in isolation, ensuring each module behaves correctly under normal and boundary conditions.
* Integration Testing: Supertest will verify correct interaction between API endpoints and the PostgreSQL database, including data flow, error handling, and authentication middleware.
* System Testing: Cypress will provide end-to-end automated testing of complete user workflows—voter registration, authentication, manifesto viewing, ballot casting, and manifesto tracking—across different browsers and devices.
* Security Testing: OWASP ZAP (Zed Attack Proxy) will be used for automated penetration testing to identify common vulnerabilities including SQL injection, XSS, CSRF, and authentication weaknesses.
* User Acceptance Testing (UAT): A structured UAT session with 30 student participants will evaluate system usability, task completion rates, and user satisfaction using standardised usability metrics (System Usability Scale).
All testing activities will be documented in a formal test plan specifying test cases, expected outcomes, acceptance criteria, and bug tracking procedures.
3.7  Methodology for System Deployment
A phased deployment strategy will minimise risk and allow iterative refinement:
* Phase 1 – Controlled Pilot: Initial deployment within a single faculty to gather real-world operational data and user feedback before broader rollout.
* Phase 2 – Full University Deployment: Expansion to all faculties following evaluation and incorporation of lessons from the pilot phase.
User training will comprise: a two-day administrator training programme on system management and audit functions; a one-day candidate training on manifesto upload, profile management, and post-election reporting; and self-service online tutorials and user guides for student voters. Post-deployment support will include a help desk, bug tracking system, automated daily backups, and ongoing performance monitoring.
